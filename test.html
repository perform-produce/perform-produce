1. Paragraph

    Bridget Moser is a performance and video artist who combines strategies associated with prop comedy, experimental theatre, absurd literature, and intuitive dance in her work. She uses everyday objects that embody the aesthetics of mainstream consumer trends as her performance props, and often references popular cinema, advertising, home decor, and influencer culture in the design of her stage environments. Moser’s work uses humor and physical perfomance to explore themes of loneliness, absurdity, existential anxiety, and consumer culture, and often makes reference to art and design history in both her video pieces and live performances. She has presented work at the Art Gallery of Ontario, Remai Modern, le Musée d’art contemporain de Montréal, and the Vancouver Art Gallery among other venues.

    BM
    Paragraph

    First, the idea for Every Room is a Waiting Room in particular goes quite a ways back. After I graduated from art school around 2009, I really didn’t know what the hell I was doing. I was living in Montreal and got an office job designing packaging for a wholesale cookware company, and I was surrounded by these stupid objects all the time that were just lying around in boxes everywhere. Looking back, I realize that a lot of my work comes from this experience and the absurdity I found in the inanimate things I was surrounded by and working with all the time. 

    As part of that cookware job, I’d occasionally travel to these international houseware trade shows. They’re super weird and I love them because of that. I got to go to the biggest one which was in Chicago — Emeril was there, the Slap Chop™ guy was there — and it was amazing. The best part for me was this massive Pantone® booth at the center that predicted the upcoming color trends in houseware design.

    The whole setup went far beyond just advertising the Color of the Year. The booth showed at least ten different color stories with eight to ten chip palettes each. It had a whole narrative constructed around each one. I became obsessed with the whole thing because it was so absurd to me that this company had found a way to market and sell the idea of color. It was so preposterous, and I found it impossible not to see it as overdrive late capitalism.

    So I’d been thinking about this Pantone® booth for years. Then finally, in 2016, when the company announced the official color that year1, they announced it as two colors in a gradient. I thought this was just so ridiculous.

    Many of us at the moment were like, this is so stupid but also so amazing. The whole marketing around the colors was this narrative about how it was 2016 and this gradient was meant to represent peace and harmony. That’s what 2016 was going to be all about. They’d decided that gender was fluid (they solved this problem for us), and they were just like, “you’re welcome.” (laughs)

    At the time, I also really wanted to engage with the idea of consumption and production cycles; with objects that were just recently becoming irrelevant in terms of the trend cycle. I was interested in working with things that were being liquidated because the new Pantone® color was about to come out, and for practical reasons, that also meant it was affordable for me to buy all of these things to work with because everything was on sale.

And so I do think you’re pointing exactly to that tension between this cold loneliness and the Pantone® of it all. There’s a meaninglessness and pointlessness to this color marketing that I really wanted to work with.

    Another thing I wanted to ask you about is your use of objects that nod to art and design history. While your work is very contemporary, it seems like you’re often gesturing to moments or figures in the history of visual culture.     Side Annotations
  21. Speaker
    BM
    Paragraph

    Definitely. I feel like prop comedy wouldn’t exist at all without Marcel Duchamp. I also think there’s a very strong link between industrialization, modern art, and contemporary comedy, which is something David Robbins goes in-depth about in his book Concrete Comedy [06]. First, we needed mass-manufactured objects to get to readymades, and then we need readymades to get to prop comedy. So in some ways, using objects in this way is speaking to a history that is inclusive of both Duchamp and Carrot Top [07].
    And I think they should be given equal weight or importance, though I don’t mind if people only see one or the other in what I do.

    I wanted to ask you about centring your own body in your work. In all your live performances as well as your videos, your body is always present. You use it to activate props like beauty products, clothes, mannequin parts, and other things associated with women’s bodies especially.
    Why is using your own body important, and how do you see yourself in the broader tradition of feminist performance art? I’ve heard you mention artists like Laurie Anderson10 and Yvonne Rainer11 in previous interviews, and would love to hear more about who you’re influenced by and what you’re trying to do now that’s different from artists who came before you.

    Side Annotations
  33. Speaker
    BM
    Paragraph

    Well, in the beginning, when I started making this kind of work, I didn’t consider my own presence particularly important. I had been making another type of work for a while, but it wasn’t very good. I think it's important for younger artists to know how much bad work you have to make in the beginning to understand what’s happening when the work starts getting better. So anyway, for me, the moment my work started getting better was when I started performing; when I started being the person to activate the things around me.

    Performance was this medium that allowed me to bring together all of the other things I was interested in but that I didn’t initially connect to my art practice. Writing is one example of something I always enjoyed but thought of as separate from visual art until I discovered that I could write things and then say them. I could dance and move which is also something I like to do. I’m interested in joke-making as well. And because I had worked with inanimate objects before, I realized that I could continue doing that in this new way using performance which also allowed me to express this interest in humor that I always had.

    Working with my own body started out of necessity because it was just me making work alone. But I recognized early on that performance actually works really well for me— it makes sense with the way my brain works more than anything else. 

    This question of the feminist tradition of performance art has come up a few times recently. And it’s funny because there is a lot of talk about thwarting expectations of how women are expected to present in the world, even in the art context, but I don’t actually think about it that much. I’m often more focused on whichever bad ideas I have and how I can bring them to life in a way that’s interesting to me.

    I feel like my work is more personal and quite selfish in the sense that I’m often thinking about what pleases me. And maybe it just makes perfect sense that what pleases me is an inherently feminist action. Maybe it’s less of a conscious act and more about what is embedded in who I am as a person, if that makes any sense.

    The result of this attitude is work that’s surprising and fresh and also very funny. Humor is clearly central to your work and its overall impact. Can you talk a little bit about humor as a tactic? How do you even begin to think about constructing a work that’s supposed to be funny?

    Side Annotations
  37. Speaker
    BM
    Paragraph

    Well, sometimes I watch things back and think, “this video is not actually that funny.” (laughs) But there are different theories about what makes something funny. In fact, there are hundreds of years of thought and writing on what constitutes a joke. One of the prevailing theories I identify with most is the incongruity theory, which comes largely from Enlightenment thinking. Depending on whose writing you read, it essentially boils down to the perception of something incongruous causing us to laugh. Immanuel Kant described laughter as “an affection arising from the sudden transformation of a strained expectation into nothing.”12 

    I’m very drawn to this definition as an artistic tool because it allows me to take these common ideas we have that seem to be inherently wrong or bad—ideas about women, ideas about race, ideas about consumption or capital—and sort of stretch them in an absurd way that reveals how empty and baseless they are. By stretching them in this absurd and hopefully humorous way, I can show how fallible and not worth holding onto they are. That works well as an artistic tool, and it’s also pleasurable. It feels good to laugh, and I think that's also important.

    But then, at the same time, I also just love to do stupid shit. So maybe it’s not always quite as high-minded as Kant’s definition suggests. (laughs)
